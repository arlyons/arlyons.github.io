Stereotype Threat

Today's Date

The research on stereotype threat and how powerful the act of reframing the context of a potentially threatening situation can be in attenuating the threat is fascinating to me. I definitely remember hearing (and sadly believing) "girls are bad at naturally bad at math." I also remember being exposed to many other "positive" stereotypes about women that were probably meant to temper the bad ones: "women are better at multi- tasking, girls are more verbal than boys at a younger age," etc. Unfortunately they just served to reinforce the idea that I was biologically incapable of being good at math, and that I am a "language- person."

Upon acceptance into DBC you are given a list of optional reading. One of the books on the list was A Mind For Numbers by Barbara Oakley. It's written for people exactly like me who suffered under the illusion that they just aren't wired for math and science and gives insight how to the inevitable confusion that comes with these subjects is just part of the learning process and not a reason to give up. I actually started to become angry reading it thinking about all missed opportunities I had growing up to learn about subjects that I found fascinating, but never even attempted because of the math involved. I also had a list of teachers that really dropped the ball in terms of understanding and motivating fellow "mathphobes." Instead, whether explicit or not, the stereotype that girls just aren't good at math was reinforced.

The extremely good news about the stereotype threat research is how little it takes to actually undermine and diffuse the perceived stereotype threats. Something as simple as taking the time before a test to let students know that gender or race won't play a part in their ability to do well on the test, or to place questions about race or gender at the end of the test rather than at the beginning is surprisingly effective in leveling performance across racial and gender lines. I hope this research becomes more well known and integrated into our educational system.
